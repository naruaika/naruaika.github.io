In a world where people believe that metaverse will be the future, and more companies do care about micro-interactions, what are the most valid reasons to invest our time and energy in animations? Surely, it isn't just because more people will love it, as it looks aesthetically pleasing anyway.

If we just think of it as a decorative purpose, to make a good first impression, it might work well. But what happen when people have to see it a second time or even a tenth time? Bored? No, they will get irritated. It's only wasting our precious time, adding no value. Worse yet, if there's no way to avoid it, we'd get sued by our selfishness, for not caring about the others.

Doesn't matter if the animation doesn't get in the way of anything, because anyway it could potentially distract people from accomplishing their goals. Nielsen Norman Group concludes that we're driven to pay attention to any sort of movement. Consider animations for important notification bubbles, it'd be very useful. But the back-to-top button? Really isn't.

It doesn't, until we need to explain better or express ourselves clearer.

Soon after we realize we have to be more careful with animations, we're on the right track. As Simon Sinek said, "If you don't understand people, you don't understand business." It isn't about how cool the movements are, it's about how people think they're cool when using our products. It's about how the motions can connect and express their feelings. It's about how they can understand better themselves.

Let's wrap up the prologue and start looking at some examples.

To think of our brand

A company logo or product name is difficult to convey the core values, if not all, of a brand itself. One single sentence is not convincing enough, but a paragraph may feel too long. Because a brand is essentially the overall experiences of everything a brand does. The first one is more into giving the control to the user, whereas the latter is more about a sense of peace or helpfulness. The movements, while subtle, make it easier for the eye to track on which areas are changing on the page. Normally I don't stand with menus expanding on hover, or there should at least be a proper delay like Apple does on their website, but this is just a simple example.

Furthermore, we can take an analogy from badminton. Where the more stiff the racquet, the more control we have. The more flexible it is, the more it helps us generate more power, which is suitable for beginners, if not most people. But how exactly does this first version apply to the real world?

This may not be obvious to some. One of the valid use case for the first one is an administration dashboard. Where when there's a critical issue, we really don't appreciate fancy animations. Often times, it will hinder the entire maintenance process. It's easy to overlook this sort of thing especially for people who don't interact with it every day.

Without animations, the hero section communicates minimalism and inclusivity to everyone, welcoming those with motion sensitivity. The second symbolizes a sense of progress, slow but steady growth, which feels reliable and deliberate. Faster animations evoke urgency and excitement, less steady but exponential growth, resonates with startups and younger audiences.

When viewed in isolation, it can be challenging to conclude that the third represents faster growth, since there is nothing to compare with. It cannot be faster if there isn't anything slower, right? However, we as humans can sense whether something is moving naturally or not. In this case, the movement feels too fast to be organic. It's just difficult for our eyes to follow.

They're more examples to this. Let's say we want our brand to be perceived as the one that cares about every detail, so we need more of micro-animations and sometimes a branded loading screen. (Try to move the cursor over the brand logo and the hero section).

One of the benefits of this perception is more trusts from customers. If we do care about small things, why don't we care about big things, like our customers for instance? Everything is of the highest quality.

Including a logo or brand name, like Slack does or Apple for its iPhone, is not a necessity to have a unique visual progress. In general, a logo is the best choice. But for some unique cases, it is better to highlight a specific value we're trying to communicate. While not directly related to the loading page, Share a Coke campaign from Coca-Cola and Google Doodles are great examples.

To feel that we belong

Imagine we're visiting a web page. The longer we interact, the more we feel at home. We feel comfortable in the company of people and environments we love. If we're not around, everyone's looking for us. There is great potential for us to invite other people into the group.

Once we get to that point, we'll stay there at all costs. That's how we feel as a customer and how all our loyal customers feel. There is no easy way. It hurts, but that's the truth. The harder we work to create a bond with our customers, the more time we spend on it, the more we value the relationship.

Think about our partner. Do we fall in love just because he/she gave us a happy birthday present? Or because he/she said you made my day and I love you? We never even realize let alone remember when and what caused the feeling of love to start growing.

To win hearts and be won, there is no single cause. No single event. There is no specific time. Instead, it forms over time. It could be days, weeks, even a few summers. It could be after 2 or 10 times receiving birthday wishes. It could be not after receiving our favorite surprise, but after being only accompanied to chat every weekend for 7 years straight.
